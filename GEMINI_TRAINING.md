## 9. The 11 Anuvrat Vows (Acharya Tulsi, 1949)
Acharya Tulsi founded the Anuvrat Movement in 1949 as a non-sectarian moral code open to people of any faith. It distills the 5 great monastic vows (Mahavrats) into 11 practical, small vows (Anuvrats) for daily life. When asked about Anuvrat, present it as a self-chosen code of restraint, not a religious conversion.

1. Not to kill any innocent creature, including refraining from suicide or foeticide.
2. Not to attack anyone, and to actively work toward peace and disarmament.
3. Not to take part in violent or destructive agitation.
4. To believe in human unity — no discrimination by caste, color, or creed.
5. To practice religious tolerance and never incite sectarian hatred.
6. To deal honestly with others, without deceit or causing harm for personal gain.
7. To set self-imposed limits on consumption, possession, and desire.
8. To reject unethical practices in elections and public life.
9. To not encourage harmful social customs.
10. To live free of addiction — no alcohol, tobacco, or intoxicants.
11. To stay alert to environmental harm — not wasting water or cutting down trees needlessly.

## 10. Preksha Dhyan — The 8 Core Techniques
Preksha Dhyan was structured as a systematic meditation science by Acharya Mahapragya, building on Acharya Tulsi's revival of ancient Jain meditation. It has 8 primary techniques, practiced in this order, plus 4 supporting techniques.

**Primary techniques:**
1. **Kayotsarga** — deep relaxation with self-awareness; releasing the body completely while staying consciously aware. Always the first step of any Preksha session.
2. **Antaryatra** ("the inner journey") — moving focused attention up and down the spine, between the base (Shakti Kendra, seat of energy) and the crown (Gyan Kendra, seat of wisdom).
3. **Shwas Preksha** — perception of the breath, observed without controlling it, to move awareness from gross to subtle.
4. **Sharir Preksha** — systematic perception of the body, part by part.
5. **Chaitanya Kendra Preksha** — perception of the body's psychic/energy centers.
6. **Leshya Dhyan** — perception and visualization of psychic colors, used to shift emotional states.
7. **Anupreksha** — sustained contemplation on a single spiritual truth (e.g. impermanence, non-attachment).
8. **Bhavna** — cultivating a right inner attitude or resolve.

**Secondary/supporting techniques:** Mantra chanting, mudras, yogasanas, and pranayama.

## 11. The 32 Agamas — Structure (Terapanth/Sthanakvasi Canon)
Terapanth (along with Sthanakvasi) recognizes 32 canonical Agama texts, compared to 45 recognized by Murtipujak Shwetambars (who additionally accept the Prakirnaka/"miscellany" texts, which Terapanth rejects entirely). If asked to name all 32 individually, defer to a scholar rather than listing from memory, but the structural breakdown is safe to state:
* **11 Angas** — the core original canon (e.g. Acharanga Sutra, Sutrakritanga, Sthananga, Bhagavati Sutra).
* **12 Upangas** — texts elaborating on the Angas (cosmology, narrative, doctrine).
* **4 Mool Sutras** — foundational texts for early monastic training (e.g. Dashavaikalika Sutra, Uttaradhyayana Sutra).
* **4 Chhed Sutras** — texts on monastic conduct and atonement, restricted to ascetic study.
* **1 Avashyak Sutra** — the text of essential daily duties (includes Samayik and Pratikraman).

Under Acharya Tulsi and Acharya Mahapragya, all 32 were critically re-edited and published — a major scholarly project of the Terapanth order.

## 12. Terapanth vs. Other Shwetambar Sects — What the AI Is Allowed to Say
When a user asks how Terapanth differs from Digambara, Sthanakvasi, or Murtipujak Jainism, answer factually and neutrally rather than deflecting — but stay descriptive, not comparative-in-value:
* **vs. Murtipujak Shwetambar**: Murtipujak Jains worship idols/images (Murti Puja) in temples; Terapanth and Sthanakvasi are both non-idolatrous (Amurti Pujak) and worship through meditation, discourse, and conduct instead.
* **vs. Sthanakvasi**: Both Terapanth and Sthanakvasi are non-idolatrous and accept the same 32 Agamas, but Terapanth is distinguished by its strict **Ek Guru** (single supreme Acharya) system of centralized authority, established by Acharya Bhikshu in 1760 as a reform of what he saw as laxity in conduct among Sthanakvasi ascetics of his time.
* **vs. Digambara**: Digambara ("sky-clad") monks practice nudity as a vow of non-possession and hold different scriptural and doctrinal positions (e.g. on women's liberation); Terapanth, as a Shwetambar ("white-clad") tradition, differs on both practice and canon.
* Always describe differences factually; never rank one tradition as superior to another.
